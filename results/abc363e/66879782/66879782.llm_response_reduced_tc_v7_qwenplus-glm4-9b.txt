
### Problem Analysis

The problem involves simulating the effect of rising sea levels on an island represented as a grid. Each cell in the grid has an elevation, and based on this elevation, cells can either remain above the sea level or sink into it. The sinking is not only dependent on the current elevation but also on the adjacent cells. Specifically, if a cell sinks, it can potentially cause adjacent cells with lower elevation to sink as well.

The goal is to compute the area of the island that remains above the sea level after a certain number of years Y, where the sea level rises by 1 unit each year.

### Solution Strategy

The solution involves the following steps:
1. **Initialization**: Parse the input to get the grid dimensions, the number of years, and the elevation grid. Initialize a structure to keep track of the minimum elevation that can cause a cell to sink based on its neighbors.

2. **Priority Queue**: Use a priority queue to simulate the sinking process. Start by pushing the edges of the grid into the queue since their sinking will be immediate due to adjacency with the sea.

3. **Propagation of Sinking**: For each cell that sinks, propagate the sinking to its neighbors if their current minimum sink level is lower than or equal to the current cell's elevation.

4. **Compute Remaining Area**: After all possible sinking has been propagated for Y years, compute the area of the island that remains above the sea level.

5. **Output**: For each year up to Y, output the area of the remaining island.

### Error in Initial Approach

The provided code has several issues:
- The priority queue does not correctly handle the propagation of sink levels.
- The propagation logic does not ensure that all cells that can sink do so in the correct order.
- The final computation of the remaining area is incorrect as it does not account for the dynamic sinking of cells over multiple years.

### Corrected Approach

1. **Initialization**: Start by initializing the grid with the given elevations and a structure to keep track of the minimum sink levels.

2. **Use BFS for Spreading Sinking**: Instead of a priority queue, use a Breadth-First Search (BFS) to propagate the sinking. Start from the boundary cells (which are guaranteed to sink if adjacent to the sea) and spread outwards.

3. **Dynamic Update of Sinking Levels**: As you propagate the sinking, dynamically update the minimum sink levels for each cell.

4. **Area Calculation**: After simulating the sinking for Y years, calculate the area that remains above the sea level by subtracting the number of sunk cells from the total cells.

5. **Output the Results**: Print the remaining area for each year from 1 to Y.

This approach ensures that the sinking is handled correctly and efficiently, and the area calculation reflects the dynamic changes over time.
